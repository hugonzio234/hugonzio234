Julius Caesar

the man who conquest the world

Julius Caesar, born in 100 BC, was a Roman general and statesman pivotal in the transition from the Roman Republic to the Roman Empire. Known for his military conquests, including the Gallic Wars, and his consolidation of power as dictator for life, Caesar's assassination in 44 BC marked a turning point in Roman history. His reforms and influence laid the foundation for imperial rule under his adopted heir, Augustus, shaping Rome's future governance and military strategies significantly.

  • 100 BC: Julius Caesar is born in Rome on July 12 or 13, into a patrician family of noble but modest lineage.
  • 84 BC: After the death of his father, Julius Caesar Strabo, Caesar becomes the head of the family at a young age.
  • 83 BC: Julius Caesar is appointed as pontifex, marking his formal entry into Roman political life.
  • 81 BC: He marries Cornelia, daughter of the prominent populist leader Lucius Cornelius Cinna. During Sulla's dictatorship, Caesar is forced to divorce Cornelia and relinquish his political ambitions.
  • 79 BC: During his journey to Rhodes, he is captured by pirates in the Aegean Sea and held captive. Despite adversity, Caesar establishes a rapport with the pirates and vows revenge upon his release.
  • 74 BC: After his release, Caesar returns to Rome and becomes actively involved in politics. He begins his ascent in the political ladder, gaining recognition for his rhetorical skills and ambition.
  • 69 BC: He is elected quaestor and serves in Hispania, where he gains initial administrative and military experience.
  • 65 BC: Caesar is elected curule aedile, responsible for organizing games and public events in Rome. He builds a reputation among the plebeians for his generosity and extravagant spectacles.
  • 63 BC: He is appointed pontifex maximus, the highest religious position in Rome, further enhancing his political and social influence.
  • 61 BC: Governor of Hispania Ulterior, Caesar continues to amass wealth and prestige while developing his military prowess in campaigns against local tribes and pirates in the western Mediterranean.
  • 59 BC: First consulship of Caesar alongside Pompey and Crassus. They form a political alliance known as the First Triumvirate, an unofficial coalition to control the Senate and political decisions in Rome.
  • 58 BC - 50 BC: Caesar begins military campaigns in Gaul, lasting nine years. During this period, he conquers and subdues much of present-day Gaul (France and Belgium), demonstrating his military and strategic genius. His victories at the Battle of Alesia (52 BC) over Vercingetorix and other Gallic tribes secure his position as Rome's foremost general.
  • 49 BC: Crossing the Rubicon River in Italy, Caesar defies the Roman Senate and initiates a civil war against Pompey and his supporters. This action is viewed as an act of war and marks the beginning of the struggle for control of Rome.
  • 48 BC: Decisive defeat of Pompey at the Battle of Pharsalus in Greece. Pompey flees to Egypt, where he is assassinated by order of Ptolemy XIII, the Pharaoh of Egypt.
  • 47 BC: Caesar pursues Pompey to Egypt and becomes involved in internal political affairs there. He meets Cleopatra and eventually restores her to the Egyptian throne, thereby consolidating Rome's influence in Egypt.
  • 46 BC: Returns to Rome and is appointed dictator perpetuo (dictator for life) by the Roman Senate, granting him absolute power over state affairs. During this time, he introduces several political and social reforms, including the Julian calendar reform, which forms the basis of the modern Gregorian calendar.
  • 44 BC: On March 15, known as the Ides of March, Julius Caesar is assassinated by a group of Roman senators led by Brutus and Cassius, who feared that his dictatorial power threatened the Roman Republic.
  • 43 BC: After Caesar's assassination, a new civil war erupts in Rome. The Second Triumvirate is formed between Octavian (future Augustus), Mark Antony, and Lepidus to avenge Caesar's death and fight against his assassins.
  • 27 BC: Conclusion of the civil wars and the beginning of the Principate under Octavian, who becomes the first Roman emperor with the name Augustus. This event marks the official beginning of the Roman Empire.
